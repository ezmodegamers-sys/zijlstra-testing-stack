Your A/B test won. So why is revenue flat?

Every testing program eventually faces the year-end question. You announced +12% here, +8% there, +40% cumulative for the year, and the P&L didn't move. Nobody stole the lift. Most of it was never real.

by Sebastian Zijlstra · more essays

This gap has a name in the research literature: the replication crisis. Ecommerce testing has quietly imported every one of its causes. The difference is what happens to a result that doesn't hold. In academia it embarrasses a journal. In ecommerce it ships to production and becomes a line in someone's promotion packet. The feedback that it never worked arrives a year later, diffused across a flat revenue chart that nobody connects back to any single decision.

Here is where the lift actually goes.

01The winner's curse eats the biggest share

Suppose a change gives you a true +2% lift, and your test only has 40% power to detect an effect that size. That describes most ecommerce tests, because traffic is finite and minimum detectable effects get chosen optimistically. The tests that do reach significance are not a random sample of reality. They are the lucky draws: the runs where random noise happened to push in the same direction as the true effect. Conditional on winning, your measured lift is systematically inflated, often by a factor of two or three when power is low. You measured +6% and announced +6%, but what you shipped was worth +2%. The other four points existed only in that one sample.

This is the default outcome of running underpowered tests and shipping only the significant ones, not some rare edge case. It is also correctable: shrinkage estimation deflates the observed effect toward what's plausible given your test's power. Reality Check computes exactly that: the honest estimate you should have announced, plus the probability the variant is better at all.

02Peeking converts noise into "winners"

A significance test controls false positives at 5% only if you look once, at a sample size you fixed in advance. Check the dashboard daily for a month and stop the first time p dips under .05, and your real false-positive rate climbs toward 25-30%. A meaningful fraction of your annual "wins" are pure noise that you caught on a good day. Noise, by definition, contributes nothing to the P&L.

The fix costs nothing: decide the sample size before the test starts, and don't call it early. Committing to that in writing is the entire point of pre-registration. Lockbox locks the hypothesis, the metric, and the stopping rule before any data exists, so there's nothing left to negotiate with afterwards.

03Your platform may be lying to you

Before any statistics, there's plumbing. If your platform assigns 53/47 when it promised 50/50 (the industry calls this sample ratio mismatch), something is systematically excluding users from one arm, and every downstream number is biased in an unknown direction. Asymmetric tracking loss, where the variant's conversion pixel fires less reliably than control's, does the same damage from the other side. These failures don't announce themselves; the dashboard renders confident-looking results either way.

The only way to know is to test the platform itself: run an A/A test, where both arms are identical, and check that nothing "wins". Once a quarter is enough. The Platform Validator runs the full battery: assignment SRM via chi-squared, tracking-loss asymmetry, and a trust score you can show whoever asks why you're not just believing the tool you pay for.

04Cumulative lift is a fiction

Even if every individual win were real, adding announced lifts is arithmetic theatre. Effects overlap: two checkout improvements draw from the same pool of hesitant buyers. They also decay, because novelty fades and competitors respond. So the honest question is not what the announced numbers sum to. It is whether baseline conversion actually moved over the year, measured in your analytics rather than your testing tool.

That reconciliation is uncomfortable precisely because no testing platform will ever build it: it audits them. The Program Ledger is that view. Every shipped winner gets logged with its claimed and honest lift, your actual monthly numbers sit alongside, and a realization rate tells you how much of what you announce tends to be real. Programs that track this number get better. Programs that don't get slide decks.

// the pattern All four failures share a structure: the error is invisible at decision time and expensive later. At the moment you decide, everything looks fine. The cost arrives months later as flat revenue, where it is deniable and easy to blame on the market.

05What a defensible program looks like

None of this requires abandoning testing. It requires running it like the measurement discipline it claims to be. Validate the platform quarterly with an A/A test. Pre-register every experiment before it collects a single visitor. Don't peek, and if you genuinely need to monitor mid-test, use methods built for that. Deflate winners before announcing them. Reconcile claimed lift against realized revenue monthly, and let the realization rate calibrate how much you trust next quarter's announcements.

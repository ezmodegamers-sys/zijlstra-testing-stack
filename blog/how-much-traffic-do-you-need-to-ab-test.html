How much traffic do you need to A/B test an online store?

Some version of this question shows up every week on r/shopify, r/CRO, and r/ecommerce: "My store does about 10,000 visitors a month. Everyone says test everything, but my tests never reach significance. Am I doing something wrong?" No. The math was against you before you started.

by Sebastian Zijlstra · more essays

Nobody selling testing software has much reason to answer this question honestly. The real answer is that below a certain amount of traffic, an A/B test can't do its one job: telling a genuine improvement apart from random luck. Not "it works a bit worse" at low traffic. It genuinely cannot tell the difference, no matter how long you leave it running. The good news is you can work out exactly where that line sits for your store in about two minutes, and there are still useful things to do on the wrong side of it.

Before any of that, two words do most of the heavy lifting in this article, so here they are in plain English.

// the two words that matter Significance is the test asking one question: is this difference real, or did one version just get lucky this week? A "significant" result is one that would be unlikely to happen by chance alone. Power is the other side of the coin: if version B really is better, how likely is your test to actually notice? A low-power test is a smoke detector with a flat battery. The fire can be perfectly real and the alarm still won't go off. Small stores nearly always run low-power tests, and that single fact explains everything below.

01Run the arithmetic before the test

How long a test needs to run comes down to four things. First, your baseline conversion rate: the share of visitors who buy today, before you change anything. Second, the size of improvement you'd care about catching, called the lift (a "10% lift" means nudging a 2.5% conversion rate up to about 2.75%, not adding ten whole percentage points). Third and fourth, how careful you want to be about two opposite mistakes: calling a dud a winner, and missing a real winner. The usual settings for those last two are "95% confidence" and "80% power," and they're the defaults built into every calculator.

Now plug in a typical small store: a 2.5% conversion rate, 10,000 visitors a month, split evenly between the current version and the new one. Here is what comes out.

To reliably catch a 10% lift, which is a genuinely good result for a typical tweak, you need roughly 128,000 visitors to pass through the test. At 10,000 a month, that is 56 weeks: more than a year with your site frozen for a single experiment. A 20% lift needs about 15 weeks. Only once you are chasing improvements of 30% or more does the timeline drop under eight weeks, and eight weeks is about the longest a test should run before other things start creeping in and muddying the result: people clearing their cookies, the seasons turning, a promotion going live.

Turn the question around and it gets starker. To catch a 10% winner inside a single month, you would need about 140,000 visitors a month. Reliably spotting small improvements is a luxury only big stores can afford. That one sentence belongs on the box of every testing tool, and it is on none of them.

02"Just run it anyway" is worse than not testing

The tempting move at low traffic is to run the test anyway and hope. Every so often the result comes back "significant" and it feels like you beat the odds. Here is the uncomfortable part. When a low-power test does scrape across the line into "significant," it is usually because luck broke your way that week, sitting on top of whatever real effect exists. So the improvement it reports is almost always too big, routinely by two or three times. Statisticians call this the winner's curse: the tests that squeak past the bar are precisely the ones that got lucky, so their numbers come out inflated. The +14% you are about to present is probably a +5% in a costume. You ship it, your real conversion rate barely budges, and six months later someone in finance asks why a year of "wins" never reached the revenue.

That is why an underpowered test that "wins" is worse than running no test at all. It turns a coin flip into false confidence, and false confidence gets budget. 03Doesn't switching to Bayesian fix this?

If you have read this far, you might be thinking there is a way around all this: switch to Bayesian statistics. It is a fair question, because a lot of the tools people admire have done exactly that. Dynamic Yield and VWO both report something like a "probability to be best" rather than the traditional p-value. (A p-value is the old significance number. Roughly, it is the chance you would see a result this good if your change actually did nothing, so a small p-value means "probably not a fluke.") If the big players switched, maybe the gloomy math above is just the wrong kind of math.

Here is what the sales pages leave out. Frequentist and Bayesian are two different languages for describing the same data, and the data is where the actual information lives. If 10,000 visitors and 250 sales cannot separate a 10% improvement from random noise, no change of language conjures the missing evidence into being. Run that thin data through a Bayesian tool and it will tell you something like "66% chance B is better." That sounds encouraging until you notice 66% is barely past a coin toss. The bad news from the chart did not vanish. It just got rewritten in a friendlier unit.

So why did good teams switch? Three genuine reasons, and "it needs less traffic" is not one of them:

  • It gives you a decision, not a pass/fail stamp. The traditional approach hands a small store a flat "not significant," which lands like failure. A Bayesian tool instead says "72% chance B wins, and if we are wrong it costs about 0.2% of conversion." You can actually make a call on that, as long as you keep remembering that 72% means 72% and not "we won."
  • It has the winner's-curse fix built in. You can tell a Bayesian tool to start out skeptical (statisticians call that starting assumption a prior): assume most changes do nothing until the data argues otherwise. That quietly drags an over-excited result back toward reality, which is the same shrinkage the last section was asking for.
  • It talks like a business, not a stats exam. "92% likely to win, worst case it costs this much" is something you can defend in a meeting. A p-value of 0.04 gets misread in nearly every meeting it has ever appeared in.

Two warnings before you treat Bayesian as a magic escape hatch. First, it does not make peeking free. Check the dashboard every day and ship the instant it reads "95%," and you will fool yourself just as thoroughly as the old method does. The friendlier framing limits the damage, it does not remove it. Second, the whole Bayesian-versus-frequentist argument is mostly a distraction. Optimizely runs a frequentist engine that handles daily peeking perfectly well. What decides whether a small store can run a trustworthy test is not which camp you join. It is sample size.

So the honest takeaway is narrower than the pitch. Bayesian methods are genuinely better at helping you decide when you are stuck with the uncertainty a small store lives with, because they hand you a considered bet instead of a yes-or-no verdict. They change what you do with a thin result. They cannot make the result any less thin. That is exactly why the honest-estimate tool here, Reality Check, is Bayesian: you give it your result and that skeptical starting point, and it returns the real odds that B beats A, in plain language anyone in the room can follow.

04What actually works at 10,000 visitors a month

The line in that chart is not fixed. Each of these shifts it in your favour, and none of them needs more visitors.

  • Test bigger, bolder changes. A small store earns a trustworthy result by testing things that can plausibly move conversion by 30% or more: the offer itself, the way pricing is presented, bundling, a properly rebuilt product page. Testing two shades of blue on a button is a game for stores with a million visitors a month.
  • Measure something that happens more often. Far more people add to cart than actually check out. If you count add-to-carts instead of completed purchases, you gather usable data much faster, often cutting the timeline by two thirds. The catch is that it is a stand-in for the thing you truly care about, so decide up front that you will trust it, instead of quietly switching to it later because it gave you the answer you wanted.
  • Pool similar pages together. One test running across all your product pages reaches a verdict where fifty separate per-page tests never would.
  • Two versions, never five. Every extra variant splits your traffic thinner and raises the odds that one of them looks like a winner by pure chance.
// the decision rule Before any test: put your numbers into a sample size calculator and read the duration. Under eight weeks, run it. Over eight weeks, change the plan: a bigger swing, an upstream metric, or no test at all. Shipping the change and watching your baseline is more honest than a test that was never going to conclude.
